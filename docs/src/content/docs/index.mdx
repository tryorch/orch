    <div class="orch-manifest-copy">
      <h2>Readable enough to review. Structured enough to recover.</h2>
      <p>The manifest stays close to the way developers already describe environments: runners, components, source, outputs, hooks, and state backend configuration.</p>
      <div class="orch-point-list">
        <div class="orch-point">Components can depend on outputs from earlier components.</div>
        <div class="orch-point">Adapters capture payloads and artifacts needed for teardown.</div>
        <div class="orch-point">State backends keep runtime facts outside the manifest.</div>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section class="orch-band">
    <div class="orch-section-head">
      <h2>Designed around lifecycle, not just launch.</h2>
      <p>Orch tracks where each component is in the run, captures the operational facts needed to destroy it, and keeps enough context to retry after interruption.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="orch-flow">
      <article>
        <span>01</span>
        <h3>Declare</h3>
        <p>Describe components, runners, inputs, outputs, and hooks.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span>02</span>
        <h3>Apply</h3>
        <p>Run components in dependency order across your runners.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span>03</span>
        <h3>Capture</h3>
        <p>Persist non-sensitive outputs, payloads, and tool artifacts.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span>04</span>
        <h3>Inspect</h3>
        <p>See component status, stage, runner, and recovery hints.</p>
      </article>
      <article>
        <span>05</span>
        <h3>Destroy</h3>
        <p>Restore artifacts and tear down in reverse state order.</p>
      </article>
    </div>
